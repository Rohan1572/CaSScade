What is Carbon Capture ?

Brief Introduction

  • Carbon capture Utilisation storage (CCUS) is a climate change migration technology where CO2 is captured from power plants and other industrial processes instead of being emitted to the atmosphere.

  • The captured Co2 is then stored in the subsurface with the goal of keeping it out of the atmosphere indefinitely and then utilizing it for several processes.

Methods of Carbon Capture

1. Post Combustion Seperation

  • Involves separation of CO2 from the flue gas emitted from thermal power plants.
  • Involves chemical adsorption of the gas in a solvent. For instance, certain amines such as monoethanolamine or ammonia (using the chilled ammonia process) can be used as solvent.
  • Flue gas is passed through the solvent at relatively low temperatures of about 40–50°C and then the CO2 is obtained by regeneration of the solvent at temperatures of more than 100°C.
  • USED BY: Coal-fired and gas-fired plants
  • ADVANTAGES: Technology more mature than other alternatives; can easily retrofit into existing plants;
  • DISADVANTAGES: Low CO2 concentration affects the capture efficiency;high initial capital investment

2. Pre Combustion Seperation

  • Involves separation of CO2 before the combustion takes place
  • Less used than post combustion
  • Can only be applied to electrical power plants and limited type of industrial plant
  • USED BY:Electricity generated plants
  • ADVANTAGES: Allows the capture of 90% of Co2,Can reduce the initial Capital cost as compared to the post combustion process
  • DISADVANTAGES: Cannot be retrofitted with the old systems, needs to build simultaneously with facility.

3. Oxy Fuel Seperation

  • The oxyfuel separation method thus involves filling of the entire combustion chamber with almost-pure oxygen and hence the emission obtained is almost entirely CO2 .
  • This is done using an air separation unit (ASU), which works on the cryogenic principle.
  • USED BY: Coal-fired and gas-fired plants which involve combustion.
  • ADVANTAGES: Very high CO2 concentration that enhances absorption efficiency; mature air separation technologies available; reduced volume of gas to be treated, hence required smaller boiler and other equipment;
  • DISADVANTAGES: High efficiency drop and energy penalty; cryogenic O2 production is costly;corrosion problem may arise.

Innovative Ideas

Carbon dioxide being an acidic oxide neutralizes with the strong base Potassium Hydroxide to give salt and water as you may commonly know. And here goes the chemistry behind it.

When carbon dioxide is exposed to the potassium hydroxide, they react with each other and undergo the neutralization reaction. the chemical reaction between the carbon dioxide and the potassium hydroxide is depicted as follows,

2KOH + CO2→K2CO3 + H2O

The potassium carbonate on further exposure to the carbon dioxide forms the potassium bicarbonate. The reaction of the formation of potassium bicarbonate is as shown below:

K2CO3 + CO2 + H2O → 2KHCO3

Quinone as CO2 Absorbent

Properties of Quinone

  • Oxidized derivatives of aromatic compounds.

  • Depending on the quinone and the site of reduction, reduction can either re-aromatise the compound or break the conjugation.

  • When quinone molecules are forced to take on extra electrons — they have a high chemical affinity for CO2 molecules and snag any that pass

What is Ion - Exchange Resin?

  • Acts as a medium for ion exchange.

  • Small (0.25–0.5 mm radius) microbeads, white or yellowish, fabricated from an organic polymer.

Types of Ion - Exchange Resins

  • strongly acidic (e.g. sodium polystyrene sulfonate)

  • strongly basic (e.g. trimethylammonium groups)

  • weakly acidic

  • weakly basic (e.g. polyethylene amine)
